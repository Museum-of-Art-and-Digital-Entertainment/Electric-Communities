Scenarios

This is a copy of an old `Democosm' document. Perhaps it is a starting place for the new Preview release of Microcosm...

Script scenarios are underway highlighting these capabilities of Microcosm, they will be designed to be modular, so a demonstration of the product can be customized for the target audience.

"Turf Sweet Turf" Scenario (Start up)

Description

The guide walks the customer through creating a simple identity/avatar, selecting TOS for their turf.

Guide explains that the turf is hosted on the local machine. Guide demonstrates object manipulation and basic control functions. Explain presentation neutrality 2D/3D. Guide walks avatar through door, and explains that the door connects automatically to another server.

Demonstrates

  • Identity security (capabilities)
  • Distributed nature of technology
  • Extensibility
  • TOS variation
  • U/I

"Fun with Friends" Scenario

Description

This is an opportunistic scenario, but should occur very early in the demo. In the last ten years of demonstrating Habitats, this scenario had the greatest impact on customers. (Requires at least 2 companion avatars, the more the better!)

When the guide encounters a group of avatars, s/he is immediately engaged in direct social interaction. The guide details the word balloon interface, shows the gesturing capabilities, telepathy, and the log.

If the customer is concerned about children in chat, guide explains the monitoring features and the log. Guide is to get the customer engaged and suggesting interactions. If the customer laughs out loud, the scenario is a success.

After a few minutes of interaction, the guide can show adding one of the other participants to address book, explaining the power of group identification. An other may offer to have the customer's avatar join a club.

The guide can choose to `follow' one of the others to the next scenario.

Demonstrates

  • Rich social environment
  • U/I
  • Logging
  • Monitoring/Security
  • Grouping & Addressbook
  • Economic exchange

"Web Integration" Scenario

Description

This is an opportunistic scenario, the guide should perform it as soon as the customer asks about web integration.

The guide moves the avatar to the posters region and explains that posters can be links to the web and vice-versa. S/he clicks on a poster to demonstrate. The guide then navigates the web browser to a link that, when clicked will move the avatar to another region in Microcosm (the next region in the demo)

Another opportunity is when another avatar is encountered: click on the avatar and select `send email to' to launch the email helper application. Guide may explain (reminds customer of ) the option of providing an email address at avatar creation time, and security implications.

Demonstrates

  • Microcosm to Web linkage
  • Microcosm to Email linkage
  • Identity and privacy issues

"Come on over!" Scenario

Description

(Requires companion avatar.) Guide is invited into a new companion's turf, to see something interesting. After some reticence, the guide agrees, attempts to follow the companion there (via imbedded URL?) The avatar encounters a TOS exception, because the companion's turf does not meet Microcosm requirements. The guide may either accept or refuse the exception, but in either case explains the options to the customer. Guide emphasizes that the turf is NOT a part of Microcosm, and is hosted still another (third) machine.

Alternate scenario (doesn't require scenario): If the guide's avatar has received an object that is not compliant with entry to his own turf, the guide can attempt to teleport home, encountering a similar TOS exception to the one explained above.

Demonstrates

  • TOS issues
  • Extensibility
  • Distributed nature of service
  • Transportable objects

"Arcade" Scenario

Description

(Requires at least on companion avatar waiting in another region of the Microcosm service)

The guide enters the Arcade region, which displays marquees for several games, one of which was set as an interest during the avatar's creation. S/he explains that this is a meeting are for people who want to talk about that particular game, and that a full support forum is available here. The guide clicks on the Forum marquee for the game, launching the we browser to get to the game BBS. The BBS show recent tournament scores, prizes, challenges, and group/club/clan listings.

The Guide explains that players can review the BBS activity while preparing to start a game. While the web browser is open, the guide turns the customers attention back to the world view window, where he click-drags on the poster again to select "Assemble players..." This game happens to support only 2 players, so a simple dialog requesting the skill level, and an editable pop down list of individuals and groups (friends, club members, or anyone) appears. After selecting "beginner" and "anyone" and clicking "OK," a message appears: "Game request sent to anyone interested in playing <game.>"

The guide explains to the customer; "The potential other players are sent a message with a click-able icon that will teleport them directly to this region."

A potential opponent sends telepathy saying "I'll be right there!" then arrives after a moment. The guide waits for the other to Join/Follow, and clicks on the game poster one last time, selecting "Start game..."

The guide explains that in the final version, a real 3rd party 2 player game starts. When the game is completed, the players are returned to a debriefing region designed in the style of the game, where they receive their scores and can discuss the outcome of the game. This region has another poster for them to jump right back into another game.

Demonstrates

  • Web integration
  • 3rd party game integration
  • Persistent identities for reputation and scoring
  • Asynchronous communications support (BBS, directories, high scores)
  • Persistent identities and reputation
  • Spontaneous game-partnering (demographics)
  • Clubs, Guilds, Clans (Tips, resources, and scores.)

"Live Performance" Scenario

Description

(Requires at least one performer and one other audience member.)

During the demonstration, a message arrives (TBD: how?) indicating that a performance is about to start at the auditorium (including an icon that will teleport avatars to the lobby.) The guide clicks on the teleport icon.

When the avatar arrives, the web browser is automatically set to display the promotional we page for this event. The guide tries to enter the auditorium, but is bounced because he doesn't have a ticket. He explains this to the customer and makes his way over to the ticket counter. He purchases a ticket and explains that Microcosm could charge tokens or real $ for events as a method of generating revenue.

(Optional sub-scenario: If another avatar is present, he could beg the ticket from the guide. The guide could use this opportunity to demonstrate the transferable nature of this particular ticket, and then purchase another.)

With ticket in hand, the guide now enters the auditorium and gets a seat right down in front (all seats can be right down in front.) The performers are just getting started. The guide explains the crowd graphic (region clones), the stage, and the active avatars down in front. The guide shows the menu items that allow him to change `rows' to join a friend. If many other people are in the auditorium, the guide may demonstrate this capability.

Demonstrates

  • Tickets (Transferable, Real $ value?)
    Potential revenue model
  • Crowds (Social Design)
  • Auto seating with friends or compatible others.
  • Scaling for large events via clone regions (Distributed communications)
  • Unique dual field of vision (Stage and Row)
  • Non-symmetrical communication (broadcast) Performers don't see individual clone interactions

"Follow me!" Scenario

Description

Early opportunistic scenario. Works great after "Fun with Friends." (Requires at least one companion, but more is better.)

The guide encounters another avatar and feigns being lost or asks the other "What's new and cool?" The other offers a tour and says to click-and-flick "Follow" while pointing at his avatar. If more than on companion is present, they all decide to follow the leader as well. As the leader travels, any poster he clicks on will also be displayed in the guide's web browser (a la Virtual Places) . The leader takes the guide/group to the next scenario.

During the trip, the guide may optionally show the telepathy interface, and show that the entire traveling group is now an option for addressing. Send a message. One of the group members should reply.

Demonstrates

  • Dynamic grouping
  • Web integration
  • Competitive advantage (Better tours than Virtual Places)
  • Group communication (telepathy to group)

"Managing Groups and Communication" Scenario

Description

(Works best with at least 2 companions nearby)

The guide indicates that groups and clubs play an important role in socialization. First, a nearby avatar is added to our list of friends by simply clicking on that avatar and selecting "Get more info." There is a button on the information dialog "Add to address book," which the guide clicks. The address book window appears with the page already filled out. The guide selects the "Friends" group, and clicks "Add to group." The guide explains that arbitrary groups can be created and avatars can belong to multiple groups.

Several avatars can be added to the Friend group. The guide explains that they now can use that group name wherever an individual avatar name would be permitted. To demonstrate, the guide sends a group telepathy message to his friends.

If one (or more) of the friends is not reachable, a message indicating this will be returned to the guide. An option to "Leave the message on an answering machine" will be presented, which the guide will not use, but explain to the customer.

(Much optional presentation material can go here explaining permissions and clubs as well, but that is TBD)

Demonstrates

  • Group management (clubs, clans, etc. )
  • Group communication
  • Distance communication (page, answering machine)

"How's my hair?" Scenario

Description

The guide decides to change his/her avatars `look.' He walks to the Costuming Shop. Lining the walls inside the shop is a wide array of clothing, accessories, masks, etc. The customer selects a style to his/her liking and the guide selects "Try this on" from the item's pie menu. A duplicate of the item is generated and automatically worn by the avatar, while a pricing message and descriptive text is displayed (a web page may even open.) The guide tries to leave the shop, but is prevented from doing do with a message from the clothing item stating that s/he must first pay for the item, or return it. The guide and the customer play with the various items until the right look is created, select `pay for outfit' while clicking on one of the items. The transaction is completed, and the guide can move on.

The guide can now demonstrate coloring and texturing as well. (TBD)

Demonstrates

  • Built-in customization
  • Advertisement through costuming (Nike sneakers?)
  • Internal economy
  • Automatic transactions (simple bots)
  • Object loaning (more TOS)
  • Colors and Textures

"He said a naughty word!" Scenario

Description

This scenario is used when the question of unsociable behavior arises. When the customer is a children's content supplier, perform this routine early in the demo. (Requires at least one companion avatar)

Controlling exposure to "unacceptable behavior" is important to many people, especially parents. The guide opens the logging window and activates the monitoring filter, while explaining that monitoring is done locally, with potential violations being reported to Microcosm as detected. After closing the dialog, the guide points out the `help' button, which can be used to manually capture a snapshot log of current activity for transmission to Microcosm.

At the guide's prompting, the other will utter a phrase that violates the Microcosm Terms of Service. The guide's machine will create an alert dialog box that asks if s/he wants to report this as a violation, ignore it, ignore and don't ask again. The guide selects `report', fills in a descriptive text field, and sends the log automatically.

Another way to control one's own environment is to add people to your "unfriendly" list. The guide demonstrates this by creating a address book page for the target avatar and choosing the "unfriendly" group (See "Managing Groups" scenario). S/he explains that this will automatically reject telepathy, follow and several other forms of contact from this avatar. Other groups can be created with more or less restrictions at any time.

Demonstrates

  • Logging & Monitoring U/I
  • TOS issues
  • Distributed security
  • Groups (bozos, enemies, etc.)

"Teleporting and the Map" Scenario

Description

The guide explains that walking is a fun way to get around Microcosm and meet new folks, but there are other ways to get about. The guide opens the map and shows the filtering features. A useful one for browsing is the "where is everyone" overlay. The guide demonstrates the "Friends" filter as well.

The guide clicks on the map and is teleported to the nearest landing pad. S/he explains the security repercussions of arbitrary point-to-point teleport (into your private office or bedroom.) Having arrival pads also helps to keep some sense of geography in the space.

Teleport addresses are URLs, and can be imbedded in all chat communications. The guide sends telepathy to a friend asking him/her to join him, supplying a URL, which appears as a link icon in the message. The friend may or may not follow the link to join.

Optional: The guide uses the web browser to find a private `turf' or another server, and teleports there via a web browser URL. Upon arrival, s/he points out that the map has changed to the map of the new server. The guide either continues with the next scenario here, or clicks on the `back world' button.

Demonstrates

  • Teleporting (from URL and map)
  • Secure but flexible movement with sense of geography
  • Map Filters (esp. groups)

"It takes two..." Scenario

Description

(Scenario requires one companion)

The guide goes to the Puzzle Region and points out that multi-user object based worlds make creating cooperative tasks as easy to create and resolve as competitive ones. The guide points at the giant boulder blocking the way to the treasure and flicks "Push." The boulder sends a message back stating that this boulder is too heavy to move alone.

The guide discusses the options with the guest, and they decide to call on friends (via telepathy) to come and help move the rock (embedding the Puzzle region URL automatically.) One ore more friends arrive, and they all push the rock aside, and collect the prize tokens.

Option: To make this scenario more entertaining, the other(s) can negotiate a price for assistance. If this is done, after the prize is collected, payment may be made. Much later, after the EC commerce protocols are complete, the negotiation can be formalized into a contract and this can be the launching point for another scenario.

Demonstrates

  • Telepathy
  • Internal cooperative/competitive tasks
  • Social skills (asking favors, negotiating)

"Playing Checkers" Scenario

Description

(Requires one companion for play, and at least one observer.)

The guide enters the Checkers region and explains that virtual versions of classic games are implemented as freely moving objects in a constrained space. The game "rules" aren't implemented here (there are dozens of variations of checkers and games played with checker pieces.) On the other hand, that button will set the board up for several traditional starting positions. The guide presses the button to set up for Draughtsman's Checkers. If there is no player present, s/he calls on his friends via group telepathy for a opponent (this will also alert others to arrive as observers.)

The guide picks up one of the checkers and decides to try to steal it, wither by putting it in the avatar's pocket or leaving the region. The effort is rejected, demonstrating the security properties of the object and the region.

The guide plays a few turns of checkers, while explaining the 2 avatar limit that prevents gathering observers from interacting with the objects. They can send messages, or they can be blocked by the region (to discourage distraction and/or coaching.) Note that it is impossible to prevent people from coaching as there are ways to communicate outside of Microcosm.

The guide thanks the opponent and the observers, and moves on to the next scenario.

Fun options: Instead of playing checkers, move them around to make a funny drawing or spell a word. Play Wolf and Goats (a very quick game with 5 pieces.) Cheat by moving the opponent's piece. Make a tower out of the pieces. Reset the board in it's different configurations.

Demonstrates

  • Ghosts
  • Open nature of objects
  • TOS for checkers pieces
  • Gathering players

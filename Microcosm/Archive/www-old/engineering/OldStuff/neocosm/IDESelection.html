IDE Selection

After careful consideration of various Integrated Development Environments, the "official" IDE for the purposes of checkin verification (build buddy-style) at Electric Communities will be Symantec Cafe.

The reasons for choosing Symantec Cafe over the other contenders include minimization of decision energy given that a full evaluation of each and every candidate would take weeks and that Symantec Cafe is the IDE of choice at our sister company, Onlive. Choosing Symantec Cafe allows us the possibility of future compatibility with Onlive development efforts, and gives us a stake in the ground from which to measure.

Note however, that in order forestall religious fervor, no one is required to use Symantec Cafe for their day-to-day development. In fact, we (RobJ and BobS) want to encourage a diversity of development environments (any of the IDEs mentioned herein, emacs and Sun JDK, you name it). It is our feeling that the more compilers that can wander over our codebase, the more likely we are to catch stupid mistakes.

The bottom line: until we find some reason to not use it, Symantec Cafe will be used to validate any and all checkins into the (new?) source code tree for new development. For anything other than that specific case, any and all tools are allowed (and encouraged). Should we find a fatal flaw in Symantec Cafe, we will dump it like a hot potato -- at which point we will want (as an organization) to have a body of experience with other development tools that will allow us to choose a better one.

    Fully half of the IDEs surveyed were eliminated early,. Two of them, IBM Visual Age and Sun's Workshop for Java, because they were painfully slow (Visual Age took over five hours to import the Cosm tree!) Cygnus' IDE, which would have easily integrated with our production automated build environment, won't be Java-capable for another 6-9 months.

    Of the remainder, nothing stands out after preliminary investigation. All of them provide solid development environments with several generations behind them. All of them are optimized to support development efforts smaller than ours (all of them use at least 128MB of virtual memory to attempt to compile the pure Java version of cosm). J++ is the only one that currently does not offer an Java-to-native compilation (Symantec Cafe never successfully built an executable, Metrowerks has not yet had the chance to try).

    J++ promises to be the best in terms of syntax directed editing (although emacs should give it a run for its money, suitably configured). Symantec Cafe has far and away the longest history behind it. Metrowerks last release was reviewed badly vis a vis other Windows editors, but the latest version has not been reviewed.

    The debugging environments for the top three IDEs need more serious investigation. Both Symantec Cafe and Microsoft J++ have had complaints against them (although the 6.0 version of J++ may have improved somewhat), and Metrowerks is only in its third round of debugging technology under Windows (although the latest version seems much improved). Some preliminary work with GDB and emacs show the GNU environment to be a strong contender (if only Cygnus was ready...)

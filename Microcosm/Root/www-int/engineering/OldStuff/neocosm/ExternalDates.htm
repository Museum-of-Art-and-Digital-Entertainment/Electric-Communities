Christmas 1999 – what does it mean to EC?
by JohnS
italicized comments by Randy, with JohnS concurrence
last modified 28 May 1998

Timeline:

 Let's work backwards and use some "real world" events and dates that will affect any product we produce. This will give us a "real" date to work to.

December 25th 1999

The moment we have all been waiting for. Thousands (millions) of people rip open a present under the tree and find that they are the proud new member of the CUSTOMER X online community. However, a few things need to happen first.

November 25th 1999

This is the day after Thanksgiving and the busiest shopping day of the year. CUSTOMER X has to have its product on the store shelves on this day. He is counting on 2 inventory "turns" or sellouts during this 30-day period to make his money. He has to ship the product first.

November 1st 1999

This is the day that the product must ship to be on shelves on the 25th. For example, Toys R Us needs at least 2 weeks just to move product from their centralized distribution centers to their individual stores. Trucking across country in winter weather can take many days. Before you can ship it you must first build it.

The service/hub [if there is one] officially opens, and anyone who can get the software can join. All NDAs and other protections will be lifted. This is the last possible flag day this year. The service rolls forward from here.

September 15th 1999

This is the day that CUSTOMER X needs to order parts for the product such as manuals, boxes and warranty cards. These take 4 weeks to produce and then you need 2 weeks to assemble the product. At this time of year the lead times my go out to 6 weeks and 4 weeks respectively if the customer is a minor player in the publishing biz. Before you can build it you need to test it.

August 1st 1999

This is the day that final testing of the completed product needs to start. This assumes only a 6-week test cycle, which assumes testing all along and rigorous testing by EC of the core. Even with these assumptions, this is an aggressive final test schedule.

This should mark the end of software Beta testing and the start of Service Pilot testing. This testing phase will focus on making sure the interaction between users and support staff are robust and tools for behavior management are fully adequate. Think of this as Beta Testing the Community. If any charging mechanisms are to be in place, this is when you turn them on. Your beta testers become paying customers, and their behavior changes to reflect this.

This is also the date that prototypes of the product need to go to magazines for review. This relates to their typical 2 month lead time per issue and that the targeted November issues will be in subscriber's hands in October to key them up for Christmas purchases. If the product is not "done" by this time it could get a bad review that would kill its sales. The major key date, however, is the E3 show.

End May 1999

The E3 show or Electronic Entertainment Expo is THE trade show for the game industry now. Companies need to show near final product here to the retail trade to get orders for the fall. If a product appears "half baked" it will not get the retail commitment it needs and consequently it will also not get the CUSTOMER X commitment it needs in terms of marketing budget and such. This is also the date that the product will start Beta test with all of its features implemented. Before you can do that you need to Alpha test it.

March 1st 1999

Alpha testing of the product should begin on this date. This would also be the absolute last date to drop in any missing features into the product. This time is what gets you a solid reception at E3. Ideally, you would want to deliver to CUSTOMER X a completed package (complete with production tools) way before this date so they can come up to speed.

January 1st 1999

Ideally, we would be spending New Year's Day with a hangover and a sense of accomplishment that we were finished with the feature set of Cosm 1.0. If we do this, we will have ample test time and we would be in a good position to handle the one feature that CUSTOMER X is insisting on including that we have not even started yet (like commerce.) This would also be the latest date that CUSTOMER X would need to start its graphics and sound production for the product.

Note: We would normally expect that content production would start significantly earlier than January. August '98 would be more realistic.

I am sure that a good "software lawyer" would be able to find devious ways to extend deadlines from the info simply stated above. These dates are real and meeting them is something we must make every effort to do.
